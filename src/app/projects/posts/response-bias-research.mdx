## Project Summary
 
This project was designed to investigate voluntary response bias through a real-world social network experiment. Two origin nodes (the researchers) sent a Google Form to individuals they had spent over an hour with in a given day. Recipients were then asked to forward the form to people they had similarly spent over an hour with the following day, and so on across multiple rounds.
 
The core hypothesis was that participation would decay with social distance — friends of the researchers would be motivated to respond out of personal connection, while individuals several degrees removed would feel little obligation and be far less likely to engage.
 
---
 
## Key Accomplishments
 
- Designed and executed a multi-round chain-referral survey experiment
- Mapped response data onto a network graph with node depth encoding
- Quantified participation drop-off across network depth levels
- Validated the hypothesis that voluntary response bias intensifies with social distance
- Presented findings as a research poster
---
 
## Hypothesis
 
Over **75%** of all responding participants would be contacted within the **first 3 rounds**, reflecting the drop-off in social obligation as distance from the origin nodes increases.
 
---
 
## Experiment Design
 
**Process**
 
Two origin participants (depth 1) each sent a Google Form to people they had interacted with for over an hour in a single day. Recipients were asked to fill out the form and share it the next day with their own daily contacts, creating a chain-referral structure across multiple depth levels.
 
**Terms**
 
- **Round / Depth:** The number of steps a form instance has traveled from the original senders. Depth 1 = the origin nodes; Depth 2 = their direct contacts; and so on.
---
 
## Results
 
**Response Rate**
 
Of the 14 responses collected, **85.71%** occurred within the first three rounds — confirming the hypothesis. The network ultimately reached **41 total individuals**, but an additional 27 named contacts chose not to respond.
 
**Participation Decay**
 
- Cumulative responses rose rapidly through the first three depth levels before growth flattened significantly.
- Less than 20% of depth-4 group members responded.
- No responses were received from any depth-5 participants.
- Despite half of all nodes failing to extend the chain to the next level, the network still reached a notable breadth, demonstrating how quickly a referral structure can scale even with degraded compliance.

**Network Visualization**
 
The response network was mapped as a node-depth graph, with node color encoding depth level (1–5). The structure visually illustrates how the network broadens initially and then fragments as participation drops off at greater depths.
 
---
 
## Analysis
 
**Voluntary Response Bias**
 
This experiment directly demonstrates voluntary response bias: individuals who chose to respond were disproportionately those with a personal connection to the experiment's origin. As social distance increased, so did indifference, causing systematic underrepresentation of deeper network nodes.
 
**Relative Respondent Statistics**
 
Examining each depth group independently reinforces the trend:
 
- Response rate per group drops sharply with depth
- Total participant percentage rises briefly at depth 2–3 (as the network expands) before declining at depth 4–5 as participation collapses
- The gap between total participants reached and total respondents widens at every successive depth level

---
 
## Takeaways
 
This project provided a hands-on demonstration of a core statistical concept — voluntary response bias — in a naturally occurring social network. The results closely matched expectations: personal connection to a study's origin is a significant driver of participation, and that effect decays predictably with social distance. The experiment also illustrated how quickly a chain-referral design can scale in reach while simultaneously becoming harder to control in terms of response quality and completeness.
