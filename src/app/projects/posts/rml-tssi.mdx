## Project Summary

Formula SAE electric vehicles are required to provide visual indication of vehicle status through a Ready To Move Light (RML) and Tractive System Status Indicator (TSSI). The existing solution used by the team consisted of a commercially available module that was physically large and not optimized for the vehicle's packaging, weight, or power requirements.

The goal of this project was to design a custom PCB capable of meeting Formula SAE visibility requirements while reducing size, weight, and power consumption. The board would contain independent Amber, Green, and Red lighting channels while interfacing directly with the vehicle's Power Distribution Unit (PDU).

A significant portion of the project focused on evaluating LED options, performing power electronics calculations, selecting an appropriate current regulation architecture, and developing the electrical design required to achieve the target light output efficiently.

Although my part in development concluded before hardware fabrication, the project progressed through component selection, electrical analysis, LED current driver design, and preliminary schematic development.

## Design Requirements

The primary design goals were:

- Replace the existing commercial RML/TSSI assembly
- Reduce overall weight, package size, and power consumption
- Meet Formula SAE visibility requirements
- Maintain consistent brightness across operating conditions
- Integrate directly with the vehicle's Power Distribution Unit

The final design incorporated three independent lighting channels as outlined by FSAE rules:

- Amber Ready To Move Light (RML)
- Green Tractive System Status Indicator (TSSI)
- Red Tractive System Status Indicator (TSSI)

Switching between lighting states was handled externally by the vehicle's PDU, allowing the PCB to focus solely on efficient light generation and current regulation.

---

## System Architecture

The board was designed around three electrically independent lighting channels.

1. Amber Channel
2. Green Channel
3. Red Channel

Each channel included:

- Dedicated power input
- Dedicated ground return
- 1-3 LED branches
- Constant current regulation circuitry
- Driver support components

This architecture allowed each indicator color to be independently controlled while maintaining precise current delivery to the LEDs.

## LED Selection and Trade Study

One of the earliest project tasks involved selecting LEDs capable of meeting brightness requirements while remaining practical from both cost and electrical perspectives.

Multiple candidate LEDs were analyzed and compared using manufacturer datasheets.

Evaluation criteria included:

- Luminous output
- Forward voltage
- Current requirements
- Availability
- Cost

Several high-performance LEDs were initially considered but ultimately rejected due to cost concerns. Other candidates were eliminated because they could not provide sufficient brightness to satisfy the intended application.

Multiple design iterations were performed before arriving at a final LED candidate that balanced performance, efficiency, and budget requirements.

The selected LED was approved by senior team members and ordered for validation testing.

---

## Optical and Electrical Analysis

A major portion of the project involved determining how many LEDs would be required to achieve the desired brightness while minimizing power consumption and cost.

Based on the target lumen output, calculations were performed to optimize:

- Number of LEDs per branch
- Number of branches per color
- Total current requirements
- Total power consumption
- Cost per implementation

Several possible configurations were evaluated before selecting a final architecture.

The final design targeted approximately 700 lumens of output for each color channel while minimizing overall component count, cost, and current requirements.

## Current Regulation Design

Determining how to regulate LED current efficiently became one of the most significant engineering challenges in the project.

**Initial Approach: Current-Limiting Resistors**

The original concept relied on resistor-based current limiting.

This approach was attractive due to its simplicity but introduced several drawbacks:

- Reduced efficiency and accuracy
- Sensitivity to supply voltage variation
- Brightness inconsistency (potential rule violation)

Further analysis showed that resistor-based regulation was not the most suitable solution for the application.

---

**Second Approach: Linear Current Regulators**

The next design iteration explored linear current regulators.

While these devices provided improved consistancy of current regulation, power dissipation calculations revealed that significant heat would be generated during operation.

Thermal concerns due to potential variation from the PDU made this approach impractical for the application and it was ultimately discarded.

---

**Final Approach: Buck Constant Current Drivers**

The final design adopted a buck-based constant current driver architecture.

Benefits included:

- High efficiency
- Reduced heat generation
- Stable LED brightness
- Accurate current control

After review with senior team members, this approach was selected as the preferred architecture for the project.

---

## Power Electronics Design

Following selection of the buck driver architecture, complete driver calculations were performed for each lighting channel.

Design calculations included:

- Output current requirements
- Current-sense resistor values
- Switching frequency targets
- Inductor selection
- Schottky diode selection
- Input capacitor sizing
- Output capacitor sizing
- Current ripple calculations

A switching frequency of approximately 500 kHz was used during driver design.

Component values were selected to satisfy current ripple and operating requirements established during design reviews while maintaining stable LED current regulation.

Separate calculations were performed for the Amber, Green, and Red channels due to differences in operating current and LED configuration.

This work established complete electrical implementations for each lighting subsystem before schematic capture began.

## Problems Faced

**Cost vs Performance**

Many LEDs capable of achieving the required brightness carried substantial cost penalties.

Selecting the final LED required balancing:

- Output strength
- Footprint size
- Efficiency + Reliability
- Cost + Availability

---

**Selecting a Current Regulation Method**

Multiple current regulation approaches were investigated before arriving at the final architecture.

This process required evaluating:

- Thermal performance
- Electrical efficiency
- Circuit complexity + size
- Ability to sustain current during voltage variations

The transition to a switching constant-current architecture significantly improved the overall design.
